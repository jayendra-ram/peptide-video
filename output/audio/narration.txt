[scene_01_intro] Peptide bonds link amino acids into proteins — from hemoglobin in your
blood to collagen in your skin. This one bond builds life's molecular
machinery. Let's see how it forms.

[scene_02_reactants] Every amino acid has two reactive ends: an electron-rich amine and an
electron-poor carbonyl. The nitrogen's lone pair makes it a nucleophile,
naturally drawn toward the carbonyl carbon.

[scene_03_orbitals] Quantum chemistry explains this through orbital overlap. The nitrogen
lone pair, the HOMO, donates electrons into the carbonyl's empty LUMO.
This frontier orbital interaction drives the reaction.

[scene_04_barrier] But there's a catch: an energy barrier of about eighty kilojoules per
mole. The Gibbs and Arrhenius equations show that without a catalyst, this
reaction is far too slow in water.

[scene_05_attack] When the barrier is overcome, nitrogen attacks the carbonyl carbon,
sharing its electrons. Pi electrons shift onto oxygen as atoms rearrange
into a tetrahedral intermediate.

[scene_06_water_loss] Proton transfers then trigger water's departure. What remains is the
new carbon-nitrogen amide bond, and the geometry flattens into a plane.

[scene_07_resonance] The resulting peptide bond is special. Resonance gives it partial
double-bond character, locking the backbone into a rigid, planar shape
that defines protein structure.

[scene_08_biology] In cells, ribosomes use ATP to forge twenty peptide bonds per second.
This chemistry also enables peptide drugs like insulin, GLP-1 agonists,
and cancer therapeutics.

[scene_09_summary] From orbital overlap to resonance stabilization, the peptide bond is
where physics meets biology. Understanding it unlocks protein engineering,
drug design, and the foundations of life.
