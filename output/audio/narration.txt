[scene_01_intro] Everyone learns that peptide bonds hold proteins together, but what is the
bond physically? Let's zoom in from amino acids down to orbitals to find out.

[scene_02_reactants] We start with two amino acids in water. The amine lone pair is electron rich,
while the carbonyl carbon is electrophilic because its pi electrons are pulled
toward the oxygen.

[scene_03_orbitals] Think of the nitrogen lone pair as the HOMO donor and the carbonyl pi star as
the LUMO acceptor. When properly aligned, these orbitals overlap to launch the
reaction.

[scene_04_barrier] Products may be favored, but there is still a substantial activation barrier.
Without catalysis, water does not allow spontaneous dehydration and bond
formation.

[scene_05_attack] The nitrogen approaches and shares density with the carbonyl carbon. Bond
orders redistribute as we climb the barrier into a tetrahedral intermediate.

[scene_06_water_loss] Proton relays sculpt which atoms carry charge. As water leaves, the new amide
grows stronger and geometry begins to flatten.

[scene_07_resonance] Resonance between the carbonyl and nitrogen grants partial double-bond
character and enforces planarity, limiting rotation along the backbone.

[scene_08_biology] Cells invest ATP to activate amino acids and position them on the ribosome,
allowing peptide bonds to form under physiological conditions.

[scene_09_summary] From orbital overlap to resonance stabilization, a peptide bond is a physical
story about electrons, energy landscapes, and biological orchestration.
